1. SUMMARY
========================================================================

PostgreSQL production database (db-prod-primary-02) experienced recurring deadlocks on the order_items and inventory tables at 14:20 UTC on September 1, 2025. The order processing and inventory update services were acquiring locks on these tables in opposite orders during high-concurrency periods. Deadlock frequency increased from 2/hour to 150/hour during a flash sale event, causing transaction failures and order processing errors.

This incident was classified as P2 severity and was assigned to
Rajesh Kumar in the Database Engineering department. The affected system was
PostgreSQL Cluster, specifically the db-prod-primary-02 component located in US-East-1 (AWS).
The incident was detected through automated monitoring and alerting systems,
and the response team was engaged within minutes of the initial alert.

========================================================================
2. BUSINESS IMPACT
========================================================================

Order processing error rate spiked to 8% during the flash sale (normal: 0.1%). Approximately 1,200 orders failed on first attempt, with 85% succeeding on automatic retry. 180 orders required manual intervention. Customer experience during the sale event was degraded.

The incident triggered the organization's major incident response process,
involving cross-functional coordination between engineering, operations, and
business stakeholders. Customer support was notified and prepared to handle

6. ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS
========================================================================

The root cause of this incident was determined to be:
Order processing and inventory update services acquiring row-level locks on order_items and inventory tables in opposite order, creating a classic deadlock cycle under high concurrency.

Further analysis confirmed that this issue had been developing over time but
was not detected by existing monitoring thresholds and alerting rules. The
failure point was within the db-prod-primary-02 component of the PostgreSQL Cluster

remediation procedures for this particular scenario.

========================================================================
7. RESOLUTION
========================================================================

The incident was resolved by the following actions: Refactored both services to acquire locks in consistent alphabetical table order (inventory first, then order_items), added advisory locks for critical sections, and implemented optimistic concurrency control for inventory updates.

The resolution was executed by Rajesh Kumar with support from the incident
response team. After applying the remediation steps, the team monitored the
system for a stabilization period to confirm that the issue was fully
resolved and no secondary or cascading failures had occurred. All affected
services were verified to be operating within normal parameters before the
incident was formally closed.

========================================================================
8. PREVENTIVE ACTIONS
========================================================================

The following preventive actions have been identified to reduce the
likelihood and impact of similar incidents in the future:

  1. Establish and enforce consistent lock ordering convention across all services
  2. Implement deadlock detection monitoring with frequency-based alerting
  3. Add advisory locks for multi-table transaction critical sections
  4. Create load testing scenario simulating flash sale concurrency levels
  5. Implement circuit breaker on order processing to handle deadlock storms

These action items will be tracked in the engineering backlog and prioritized
based on risk assessment and business impact analysis.
